MRB Website Optimisation Audit
Diagnosis
Right now the site reads as a premium UEFN / Fortnite studio that builds creator-led worlds, sells related digital products, and takes custom work. It appears to be for creators first, with brands and custom clients present but less clearly courted, and the shop feels like a legitimate secondary path rather than the main event. The action it wants is some version of contact or booking, but that intent is split across too many labels and too many levels of commitment. So yes: clarity is the headline issue, because the current site communicates “specialist UEFN studio” more strongly than “premium studio for creator-led launches,” and that weakens the halo you actually want.

The most damaging part is not that the site leads with creators. That part is correct. The problem is that the current homepage often sounds like it is describing a category deck or internal content strategy rather than making a sharp commercial case to a premium buyer. That is why the hero feels flatter than it should and why the rest of the page never fully recovers.

Positioning execution
Canada Goose executes aspirational-tip positioning by making the brand world feel like a campaign first and a catalogue second. The current homepage surfaces “Shop Snow Goose STORIES” and “Snow Goose Has Landed,” while the Snow Goose story pages expand that into heritage, creative direction, and campaign personalities, then let shopping happen through collection CTAs and embedded product cards. What it deliberately does not do is explain the full product range or flatten itself into generic outerwear merchandising above the fold.

Red Bull is even more extreme. The homepage foregrounds events, stories and culture, with drinks present but literally subordinated to the wider media ecosystem, and the Events and Athletes pages extend that world into calendars, profiles and content verticals. What it deliberately does not do is lead by selling cans. The product gets its authority from the cultural machine around it.

Patagonia splits the difference. Its current navigation makes sport, activism and stories first-class territories; the Climbing path pairs product, stories, guarantees and activism infrastructure rather than behaving like pure retail; and Patagonia Action Works gives the brand a mission layer beyond apparel. What it does not do is pretend all buyers are identical. Commuter, climber and activist can all find a path, but the site still tells you what the brand admires most.

Arc'teryx is currently more commerce-forward than Red Bull or Patagonia, but it still protects the aspirational tip through event, story and academy layers. The homepage moves between seasonal product, community events like Queer Ascent, story links, and Arc’teryx Academy, while the story and academy pages deepen the outdoor culture around the gear. What it does not do is reduce the brand to a jacket grid.

Rapha sells a cycling world, not just clothing. The homepage mixes product, club membership, sustainability and editorial stories; the RCC membership page sells rides, events and belonging; and the story pages keep the culture machine moving. What it deliberately avoids is commodity e-commerce behaviour. Even when it is selling kit, it is selling identity and participation.

On turns collaboration, athletes, stories and innovation into the halo that sells the wider catalogue. The homepage navigation gives equal weight to Shop, Explore, Athletes, OFF Stories and Innovation; the Athletes page presents sport categories, films and gear as one system; and OFF Stories routinely pairs editorial content with shop paths. What it does not do is act like a plain shoe shelf.

That is the real playbook for MRB: the homepage should not be a balanced explanation of all three business lines. It should be a desirability engine centred on creator-led launches, with enough proof that brands and custom clients immediately understand the transfer. Shopping and service taxonomy should remain easy to find, but they should not lead. The references that map most cleanly to a premium studio version of this are Linear and Vercel on the product side, and AREA 17, Instrument and Ueno on the studio side: they lead with a big thesis, then immediately justify it with product proof or client proof, and only after that do they expand into capabilities.

Within MRB’s current structure, the emphasis shift is straightforward. Foreground the hero, one flagship creator-launch proof block, and the production-path section with the glowing line. De-emphasise homepage FAQ/legal detail, generic service enumeration, and any copy that talks about “premium studio positioning” instead of client outcomes. Keep the shop on the homepage, but make it read as “these tools come from the same launch discipline” rather than “here are more things we sell.” On the current site, the homepage already says this in principle, but it spends too much time explaining the philosophy instead of proving it.

The proof artifact you need is not a portfolio gallery. It is a launch dossier: one compact proof module that shows the creator or client context, the hook, what MRB built, what launch assets were supplied, and one piece of outcome evidence or operational proof. The reason this works is that it sells the transferable thing: not “they can make a fun world,” but “they can take a public-facing interactive launch from hook to handoff.” AREA 17’s OpenAI case study works exactly this way by moving from audience expansion to trust, design language and scalable platform; Vercel and Stripe do the same with customer metrics and outcome stories. MRB needs a smaller, sharper version of that on the homepage and a fuller version on the Work page.

The Canada Goose model breaks down in four places. First, if the aspirational world is too rarefied, ordinary buyers self-disqualify. Second, if the brand has not yet earned the halo, the whole thing feels like cosplay. Third, if the secondary paths feel like leftovers, the funnel leaks. Fourth, if the proof is too vague, brands do not infer competence; they infer atmosphere. Your current site is most exposed to the second and fourth risks, because too much of the “work” is still abstract and unnamed. The fix is not to soften creator-led positioning; it is to make the creator proof more concrete and more transferable.

Direction
Direction one: launch dossier noir. Dark editorial base, one luminous visual thread, and concrete proof blocks that feel like launch evidence rather than portfolio filler. Study Linear’s homepage hero and feature pacing, AREA 17’s OpenAI case study structure, and Patagonia’s sport pages where a single world carries product, story and confidence cues together. Effort is low-to-medium, risk is low, and audience fit is highest because it keeps your bones while making the site feel rarer and more useful. This is the right direction.

Direction two: expedition luxury. Heavier atmosphere, more campaign stills, more fashion-editorial restraint, closer to Canada Goose, Arc’teryx and Rapha. This can look expensive fast, but it needs stronger imagery than MRB currently shows and it risks drifting into mood without enough proof. Effort is medium, risk is medium, and audience fit is decent for creators but weaker for hard-nosed custom clients who want evidence quickly.

Direction three: product-ops premium. Sharper SaaS influence, more metrics, tighter modules, more “system” cues, closer to Vercel, Resend and Stripe. This will convert better than a pure studio-art direction, but it can also iron out the mystique that makes creator-launch positioning special. Effort is low-to-medium, risk is medium, and audience fit is strongest for brands and developers, weaker for creators if you overdo it.

I would ignore the temptation to go full experiential-agency here. I did visit resn.co and activetheory.net as requested, but resn.co returned a 502 in the fetch and activetheory.net only exposed a JavaScript placeholder, so neither is a reliable optimisation model for this round anyway. More importantly, that genre is too likely to impress and under-convert for a small team service business.

Everything below assumes launch dossier noir.

Copy audit and rewrites
The homepage copy is strongest when it talks about the launch moment and weakest when it lapses into premium-sounding abstractions or meta commentary. The site keeps circling the same handful of words and concepts — creator, premium, launch, hook, proof, polished, readable, support — across Home, Services, About and Shop, so the copy starts eating its own tail instead of building momentum.

Hero. Current: “Game worlds built…”

Here are five angles that are materially stronger than the current hero because each says something a generic studio would not say.

Benefit-led
Headline: Build a game your audience understands before the clip ends.
Subhead: MRB builds creator-led worlds with a clear hook, strong reaction beats and the launch pack to get them live properly.

Outcome-led
Headline: Turn a creator brief into a game worth launching to an audience.
Subhead: Concept, systems, assets, UI and launch support for worlds that read fast on camera and hold up once players arrive.

Contrarian / POV
Headline: Most creator games die in the explanation.
Subhead: MRB builds the kind that land on frame one: clear hook, good footage, clean handoff, proper launch support.

Specific-proof
Headline: Creator-led game launches, built from hook to handoff.
Subhead: MRB specialises in public-facing playable launches: the world itself, the systems under it, and the assets a creator needs to ship it.

So you can
Headline: We build creator-led launches so you can go live with something worth filming.
Subhead: The goal is not more features. The goal is a world your audience gets quickly, reacts to instantly, and remembers after the stream ends.

My recommendation is the contrarian version. It has the most bite, it is still dry rather than hypey, and it immediately introduces a point of view the site currently lacks. It also solves the clarity problem fast: creators feel seen, brands understand the bar, and the CTA can now be universal.

What MRB builds. Current: “Interactive worlds shaped…”

Rewrite:
Games for creators have two jobs: they have to play well, and they have to read well on camera. MRB builds around the thing viewers notice first — the hook, the loop, the reveal, the reaction — then makes sure the systems, UI and launch materials back it up.

Why it beats the original: It replaces generic “interactive worlds” language with a concrete commercial truth: on-camera readability is part of the product, not a nice extra.

Selected direction. Current: “Lead with one…”

Delete this section as written. It is bad because it reads like an internal note about homepage strategy accidentally shipped to the public.

Replace it with:
Featured launch
One project. One hook. One proof trail. Show the world, what MRB built, what shipped with it, and what happened next. If the flagship case is strong enough, it will sell the services and the shop without explaining either to death.

Why it beats the original: Proof is persuasive; self-commentary is not.

Creator systems. Current: “survive the launch”

Rewrite:
A creator launch is not finished when the build works. It is finished when the creator knows what to show, what to say, what files they have, and what happens after day one. MRB plans the gameplay, the interface states, the reward logic and the launch pack as one job.

Why it beats the original: It translates “systems” into buyer language and makes launch support feel operational rather than airy.

From idea to launch. Current: “visible momentum”

Rewrite:
From brief to first playable, the path stays visible: audience fit, content angle, prototype, launch handoff. Keep the glowing line. Tighten the words around it so each step says what changes and what the client gets.

Why it beats the original: The visual device is already good; the copy should stop ornamenting it and start clarifying it.

Separate paths. Current: “premium studio first”

Rewrite:
Custom work is for launches and bespoke systems. The shop is for teams who only need the reusable parts. Start with services if the world is unique. Start with the shop if the problem is narrower.

Why it beats the original: It gives each path a job instead of restating brand philosophy.

Homepage FAQ. Current: “clear answers before”

Rewrite:
Before you book: yes, MRB handles custom builds; yes, licensing is clear; yes, discovery starts with audience, deadline and scope. Keep two short questions here, then push everything else to the full FAQ.

Why it beats the original: Homepage FAQs should remove friction, not recreate the FAQ page in miniature.

Final CTA. Current: “built for them”

Rewrite:
If you have an audience, a deadline and a real budget, start here. MRB takes discovery calls for creator launches, branded worlds and bespoke systems.

Why it beats the original: It qualifies the lead, broadens the secondary audience without diluting creator positioning, and makes the next step feel adult.

Across the secondary pages, the same problem repeats. The Work page talks about “proving the studio image” instead of proving competence, the Services page still leans on broad studio language, and the About page repeats philosophy words you already used on the homepage. Tightening those headers matters because premium buyers do click through, and every secondary page should sound like the same sharp firm, not like a different draft.

Design and trust audit
Hero impact is missing proof, not polish. The current hero has a decent thesis, but it does not hit hard because it offers no concrete world, no named proof, no visible deliverable, and no obvious transfer value in the first three seconds. It says “premium creator-led game launches,” then immediately falls back into a generic description of polished playable experiences. The fix is to put a real launch artefact in the hero: one still or short loop from your strongest project, plus three proof pills under the subhead such as platform, scope and launch asset. Minimum viable change: replace the anonymous hero visual with one real build still and add three tiny proof pills. Better version: use a silent 6–8 second loop that shows the world, one UI state and one launch asset transition.

Every section looks the same because the page keeps using the same rhetorical unit. On MRB, module after module follows the same pattern: label, heading, short paragraph, three support blocks. That is exactly why the page feels edited in the wrong way — not tight, but uniform. Premium sites solve this with rhythm changes: Linear jumps from giant live demo to short principles to a numbered workflow module; Vercel moves from category claim to customer outcome strip to tabbed use cases to infrastructure detail; Resend goes from hero to code surface to test-mode panel to editor; Stripe breaks the flow with a giant stats band and then case-story proof. Minimum viable change: keep the section order, but make at least three section types visually distinct — hero as proof-led, flagship case as asymmetric feature card, separate paths as a compact split module. Better version: add one mixed-light section, one oversized proof section and one compressed footer zone so the page has tempo.

The dark mode is too flat because it behaves like colour, not like lighting. The best dark references are not just dark; they have spatial logic. Resend literally uses a floor plane and a light ray. Linear lets bright product surfaces glow against a near-black field. Vercel keeps sharp white contrast and controlled panel tones. Stripe earns contrast by inserting a brighter stats world inside a darker commercial frame. MRB’s dark aesthetic is directionally right, but it needs more surface hierarchy: near-black page background, charcoal cards, a slightly lighter proof stage, thin borders, and one disciplined accent glow. Minimum viable change: introduce three dark values, one restrained cool accent, subtle texture, and brighter proof panels. Better version: give each major section its own lighting logic while keeping the same palette family.

Trust is being lost on the Work page. The project names are generic, the proof is mostly promised rather than shown, and the “View preview” path in the fetched version I inspected did not open a deeper case study layer; it returned the same Work page. That makes the whole proof system feel placeholder-ish, even though the site is trying to sound premium. Minimum viable change: turn one featured project into a real dossier on the existing Work page with concrete nouns, concrete deliverables and one actual result or testimonial. Better version: standardise three dossier cards on the Work page using the same template. A dedicated matrix of separate case-study pages would likely be stronger, but that is out of scope for this round given the locked architecture.

Motion should reinforce meaning, not just add gloss. You already have the right signature with the glowing progress line. Extend it in small ways. Let CTA borders carry a faint travelling light on hover. Let work cards reveal one extra line of metadata on hover. Let the nodes on the production line brighten as they enter view. Let the hero still breathe with a very slow zoom rather than a flashy stunt. Minimum viable change: CSS-only hover depth, button light sweep, and section-enter glow states on the line. Better version: one silent hero loop and one subtle scroll-synchronised highlight state. Anything that needs bespoke WebGL or custom motion engineering is out of scope.

Hierarchy needs to be more ruthless. The site currently gives too much space to labels, too little to actual proof, and too much visual equality to primary and secondary actions. Make the H1 bigger. Reduce the number of tiny descriptive chips above the fold. Tighten body widths. Make the primary CTA the obvious visual winner. On mobile, shorten support phrases into proof pills and let only one button dominate each decision point. A discreet sticky mobile CTA is worth A/B testing, but only after the main CTA language is unified.

CTA and conversion
Yes: MRB should have one dominant CTA. Both University.com and The Real World are crude in tone, but mechanically they do one thing very well: they repeat the same core action again and again, surround it with proof, and remove ambiguity about what the site wants you to do. MRB currently asks visitors to book, get in touch, view work, plan the launch, book around the project, and contact the studio. That is too many asks for a homepage that should feel intentional.

The site-wide primary CTA should be Book a discovery call. That is the cleanest phrase because it still fits the creator-led homepage while admitting brands and bespoke clients without making them feel like side cases. “Book a creator call” is too narrow once someone hits Services or Contact, and “Get in contact” is too weak. Use “See selected work” as the consistent secondary CTA everywhere.

The primary CTA should live in the header, in the hero, once immediately after the flagship proof block, once after the glowing production-path section, and once in the final CTA. Same words every time. Same visual treatment every time. Solid button for the primary action, quiet outline or text button for the secondary action. That is the conversion mechanic worth borrowing from University.com and The Real World: not the hype, but the singularity.

The urgency and scarcity mechanics that apply here are the adult versions, not the sleazy ones. Use capacity, timing, and fit, not countdowns. “Taking on two new custom projects this month” works if true. “Next discovery slots available in May” works if true. “Best fit for teams with a budget and a launch deadline” works if true. The site already has one useful trust mechanic — a response-time expectation — but it is buried on FAQ and Contact. Surface that near the main CTA instead of inventing fake scarcity.

The missing trust signals, in priority order, are clear. First, one real flagship proof artifact near the hero. Second, a compact creator/client roster or named collaborator strip. Third, a concrete founder-level trust layer: the fact that MRB is based in Stockholm, has direct founder contact, and replies within a stated window is good; it is just not surfaced as confidence-building copy. Fourth, one line of specialisation proof such as “working in UEFN since launch” lower on the page, not as the headline thesis. Fifth, one crisp ownership and handoff summary near the first CTA. Sixth, visible update cadence or recency on proof blocks so the work does not feel archival or theoretical.

Premium playbook
These are the small moves worth stealing.

Linear: the hero is a live artefact, not a slogan floating in space. Apply that by putting a real launch world or launch sequence directly into MRB’s hero. Study: Linear homepage hero and first proof block.

Vercel: customer outcomes appear immediately under the main claim, so the category promise gets grounded fast. Apply that by adding a three-item proof strip under MRB’s hero: project count, delivery scope, and one hard result if you have one. Study: Vercel homepage outcome row under the hero.

Resend: the dark page has a clear light source and surface hierarchy, so it feels spatial rather than muddy. Apply that by giving MRB one lighting logic across the homepage: dark background, brighter proof stage, one cool glow. Study: Resend homepage hero, floor background and light ray.

Stripe: one giant stats band changes the rhythm and creates immediate institutional weight. Apply that by inserting one proof band after MRB’s featured launch section. If you do not have big numbers, use three concrete operational facts instead. Study: Stripe homepage “backbone of global commerce” section.

AREA 17: the homepage opens with a belief statement and immediately backs it up with named client proof. Apply that by putting a small collaborator roster below MRB’s thesis. Study: AREA 17 homepage hero and client list.

AREA 17’s OpenAI case: the project is framed through audience, trust, design and platform — a buyer can see how the team thinks, not just what they made. Apply that as MRB’s work-page case-study template. Study: AREA 17’s OpenAI case.

Instrument: a slim client roster plus recent recognition quickly says “this is not a small anonymous shop.” Apply the roster logic, not the awards logic unless you genuinely have awards worth mentioning. Study: Instrument homepage client roster and recent recognition.

Ueno: a giant client marquee creates instant weight with almost no explanation. Apply a restrained version only if the names are real and impressive enough; otherwise skip it. Study: Ueno homepage client marquee.

Red Bull: culture, athletes, events and stories create a halo well beyond the drink. Apply that by framing creator work as a launch ecosystem — world, footage, update path, audience moment — rather than as isolated builds. Study: Red Bull homepage plus Athletes and Events pages.

Rapha: the club turns content, belonging and commerce into one coherent system. Apply that by making MRB’s creator-launch offer feel like a complete package, not just contract development. Study: Rapha homepage RCC block and RCC membership page.

On: editorial content and athlete identity directly bridge into shop paths. Apply that by making every proof block offer both “see the work” and “see the system/shop” routes. Study: On Athletes and OFF Stories pages.

Patagonia: sport pages mix story, product and confidence signals like guarantee and activism without losing coherence. Apply that by making Services, Shop and FAQ feel like adjacent rooms in the same house, not leftover utility pages. Study: Patagonia Climbing and related product/confidence sections.

Arc’teryx: events and academy pages extend the halo beyond shopping. Apply that by giving MRB’s creator-launch work a public-facing “moment” frame — brief, launch, updates — not just build specs. Study: Arc’teryx homepage event modules and academy pages.

Arc from The Browser Company: the headline is opinionated and the testimonials are short, human and specific. Apply that by giving MRB one sharper POV sentence and one or two short client lines instead of more studio-safe abstraction. Study: Arc homepage hero and social proof section.

Coupling and priority list
The key copy-design couplings are not optional. If you rewrite the hero to be more specific, you need a proof artefact beside it; otherwise the new copy feels unsupported. If you delete the meta “selected direction” language, you need a flagship launch card to fill that visual slot. If you unify the CTA, you need to update button hierarchy across Home, Work, Services and Contact at the same time or the inconsistency stays visible. If you compress the homepage FAQ, you need to rebalance spacing so the footer decision zone feels calmer rather than emptier. If you make the Work page more concrete, you need metadata rows or proof chips to hold the specificity. And if you want the shop to benefit from the halo, the shop teaser needs the same visual language as the flagship proof, not a generic product-grid treatment.
